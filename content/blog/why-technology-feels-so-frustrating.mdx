You just want to pay your utility bill online. Simple, right?

Except the website won't accept your password. The one you *know* is correct. After three attempts, you're locked out. Now you need to reset it. But the reset email isn't coming. You check spam. Nothing. You try again. Still nothing. Twenty minutes later, you're on hold with customer service, listening to elevator music, wondering why something this simple has to be this hard.

**You're not alone. And you're not wrong to be frustrated.**

## It's Not You—It's Them

Here's something the tech industry doesn't want to admit: **most technology is badly designed**.

Not because the people building it are incompetent, but because they're building for themselves—young developers who grew up with smartphones and think everyone understands terms like "two-factor authentication" and "cookies."

They design systems that assume:
- You remember 47 different passwords
- You have your phone with you 24/7 for "verification codes"
- You understand why a website needs to know your location
- You're comfortable clicking "Allow" on pop-ups you don't understand

**None of this is reasonable.** And none of it is your fault.

## The Real Problems

### 1. **Password Insanity**

Every website wants a different password. One requires a capital letter, number, and symbol. Another won't accept symbols. A third insists it must be exactly 8 characters (no more, no less).

Your bank forces you to change it every 90 days. But you can't reuse any of your last 10 passwords. So you end up with `BankPassword1`, `BankPassword2`, `BankPassword3`... which isn't secure anyway.

**The truth?** Even security experts gave up memorizing passwords years ago. They use password managers. More on that in a moment.

### 2. **Customer Service Nightmares**

Your internet is out. You call your ISP. After 30 minutes on hold, you reach someone reading from a script:

"Have you tried unplugging your router?"

Yes. Obviously.

"Let me escalate this to our technical team."

Another hold. Another transfer. Another person asking the same questions.

Here's what they won't tell you: **Most ISP support staff can't actually fix your problem**. They're following a flowchart. Real technicians exist, but you have to fight through three layers to reach them.

### 3. **Web Forms From Hell**

You're filling out a form. You get to the end. You click Submit.

"Error: Please enter a valid phone number."

You entered `(555) 123-4567`. It wants `555-123-4567`. Or maybe `5551234567`. No parentheses. Or maybe *only* parentheses. Who knows? The error message certainly doesn't say.

You try again. New error: "Session expired. Please start over."

**This is terrible design.** A well-built form would accept any reasonable format and figure it out. But that requires actual thought and testing.

### 4. **Mandatory Account Creation**

You just want to buy a $12 item. But first:
- Create an account
- Verify your email
- Set a password (with their arbitrary rules)
- Answer security questions
- Agree to 47 pages of terms you'll never read
- Accept cookies (what even are cookies?)

Then you can finally check out. Except now it wants you to download their app.

**Why?** Because every company wants your data. They want to send you marketing emails. They want to track your behavior. None of this benefits you—it's entirely for them.

## What You Can Actually Do

### Get a Password Manager

I know, I know—another piece of technology. But hear me out.

A password manager is like a secure digital notebook that:
- Generates strong, random passwords for every site
- Fills them in automatically when you log in
- Only requires you to remember *one* master password

**Good options:**
- **1Password** ($3/month, works everywhere, great support)
- **Bitwarden** (free or $10/year, open-source)
- **Built-in browser password managers** (Chrome, Firefox, Safari—free but less secure)

Yes, it takes an hour to set up. Yes, you'll need help the first time. But after that? You'll never type another password again.

### Write Down Your Actual Passwords

I'm serious. Security experts will cringe, but here's reality:

If you're not a spy or managing state secrets, **a notebook in your desk is fine**. Much better than:
- Using the same password everywhere
- Writing it on a sticky note on your monitor
- Resetting it every single time

Just keep it somewhere safe. Don't photograph it with your phone. Don't email it to yourself.

### Know When to Demand a Human

ISP giving you the runaround? Here's the script:

*"I've already tried basic troubleshooting. I need to speak with a Level 2 technician, please."*

Refuse to repeat information you've already given. If they push back:

*"I understand you have a process. I've been a customer for X years. Please escalate this."*

Polite but firm. Don't get angry at the person on the phone—they don't make the rules. But don't accept endless transfers either.

### Use Autofill—Carefully

Modern browsers can fill in forms for you. In Chrome/Firefox/Safari, save your address and payment info once. The browser will offer to fill it in next time.

**Important:** Only do this on your personal devices, not public computers.

### Ask for Help—Real Help

If you have a tech-savvy friend, family member, or neighbor, ask them to:
- Set up a password manager for you
- Show you how to save passwords in your browser
- Walk you through two-factor authentication once
- Organize your important login info

Offer to buy them lunch. Most tech folks love helping when they're actually appreciated (instead of treated like free tech support).

### Vote With Your Wallet

Company making things unnecessarily difficult? Stop using them.

- **Banks** should offer real phone support without phone trees
- **Utilities** should accept payments without creating accounts
- **Online stores** should allow guest checkout

If a company treats you like an idiot, they don't deserve your business. Leave a review. Tell friends. Switch providers when you can.

## It's Going to Get Worse Before It Gets Better

I wish I had better news. The tech industry is doubling down on:
- Apps for everything (even when websites work fine)
- "AI chatbots" that can't answer real questions
- Biometric authentication (face scans, fingerprints)
- Cryptocurrency and NFTs (don't ask)

But here's the thing: **you don't have to participate in everything**.

You can:
- Refuse to download apps
- Demand email support instead of chatbots
- Stick with passwords instead of face scans
- Avoid companies pushing the latest gimmick

Technology should work for you, not against you. Companies forget that.

## A Note to the Tech Industry

If you're a developer or designer reading this: please, **test your work on your parents**.

Not your tech-savvy friends. Your actual parents. Or your non-technical aunt. Or your neighbor who still has a flip phone.

If they can't use it without help, you built it wrong.

"User-friendly" doesn't mean "friendly to users like you." It means friendly to everyone.

## You're Not Too Old for Technology

Let me be clear about something: **this is not a generational issue**.

I've met 70-year-olds who code. I've met 25-year-olds who can't figure out email.

The difference isn't age—it's whether someone took the time to explain things without condescension. And whether the technology was designed to actually be usable.

You're not "bad with computers." You're dealing with badly designed computers.
